03 — Itinerary

Where will you actually go?

04 — Hotel

A neighborhood built for the thrifty traveler.

Minamisenju is famous in Tokyo for one thing: cheap, decent rooms. The streets between the station and the river hold capsule hotels and budget business hotels at prices the rest of the city forgot. A bed for the night, an early train in the morning. That's the deal.

A fridge, a TV from another decade, hangers on the wall. Yours for ¥3,500 a night. Most budget rooms here are exactly this size — small, clean, a little dated, and quiet. The trick is not to expect more, and to enjoy that you didn't pay for it.

07 — Culture

Small things, huge difference.

Japanese hospitality is extraordinary — and mostly one-directional. No one will correct you. They'll smile, accommodate, and quietly note the breach. Get these right and you'll be treated like someone who cared enough to try.

You won't get it all right. That's fine. Effort alone earns enormous goodwill. What follows are the dozen rules worth knowing — the rest you'll pick up as you go.

General conduct

The baseline expectations, everywhere.

✓
Bow slightly instead of handshaking. Depth and duration convey respect — a small head nod is fine for casual situations.
✓
Carry your trash. Public bins are rare. Pack a small bag for wrappers and coffee cups until you find one (often at konbini or stations).
✓
Cash still rules outside cities. Many temples, small shops, ryokan, and taxis refuse cards. ATMs at 7-Eleven accept foreign cards.
✕
Don't tip. It's not just unnecessary — it can be confusing or even insulting. Exceptional service is considered standard.
✕
Don't eat while walking. Street food stalls are an exception — eat near them, then walk on.

Dining

The meal has its own choreography.

✓
Say itadakimasu before eating, gochisōsama after. Even solo, it's done.
✓
Slurping noodles is expected — it cools them and signals enjoyment. Quietly eating ramen reads as odd.
✓
Wait to be seated. Even in casual places. A host will gesture where to go.
✕
Never stick chopsticks upright in rice, or pass food chopstick-to-chopstick. Both mirror funeral rituals.
✕
Don't pour your own drink in a group. Pour for others; they'll pour for you. It's a gentle rhythm of attention.

Onsen & bathing

Public bathing is sacred and strict.

✓
Wash thoroughly first — sitting at the shower stalls, soap and rinse completely, then enter the bath.
✓
Small towel stays out of the water. Fold it on your head, or place it on the ledge.
✓
Tie up long hair so it doesn't touch the water.
✕
Tattoos are often banned. Many onsen still refuse entry. Tattoo-friendly onsen exist and are easy to Google ahead of time.
✕
No swimsuits in traditional onsen. Full nudity is the norm. Private-bath ryokan rooms are an alternative if uncomfortable.

Trains & public spaces

Silence is the default.

✓
Set phones to silent — "manner mode." No calls on trains. Texting is fine.
✓
Queue precisely at the marked spots on the platform. Let passengers exit first.
✓
Priority seats exist for a reason. Give them up for elderly, pregnant, injured — or don't sit there.
✕
Don't eat on local trains (Shinkansen is the exception — onboard bentō are a ritual).

Accommodation

Especially in ryokan, small things matter.

✓
Remove shoes at the entrance. If you see a step-up (genkan), shoes off. Slippers provided.
✓
Separate toilet slippers exist in many accommodations. Swap into them in the bathroom, back out after.
✓
Ryokan curfews are real. Many lock doors around 10–11pm. Check in advance if you're a night owl.
✕
Don't walk on tatami with slippers. Socks or barefoot only.

Shrines & temples

Active places of worship, not museums.

✓
At a shrine: bow once at the torii, purify hands and mouth at the chōzuya, then at the offering box: bow twice, clap twice, pray, bow once more.
✓
At a temple: hands together, silent. No clapping.
✓
A small coin offering is traditional — ¥5 (go-en) is considered lucky, as the word also means "connection."
✕
No flash photography inside, and often no photos at all in main halls. Watch for signs.
08 — Packing

Pack for the Japan you'll actually meet.

Two truths: you'll walk more than you think (15–25km days are normal), and Japan has everything you forgot. Still, a few things are genuinely worth bringing from home.

You will walk a lot. Pack light, pack comfortable. Pick the season below. The list adjusts. Nothing on it is a guess — it's what gets used.
What you can leave at home
  • Toiletries — every konbini has what you need, often better.
  • Heavy books or guidebooks — Google Maps, the Japan Travel app, and offline Wikipedia cover 95%.
  • Umbrellas — ¥500 at any konbini when it rains. Everyone has one.
  • Formal wear — unless you have dinner plans that require it, keep it casual.
